Guidance on the worker classification: Employee or self-employed

Content has been developed to provide guidance only and it does not replace the law. No changes were made to the existing legislative requirement.

On this page

  • Factors to consider

Factors to consider

You need to consider multiple factors to determine if the worker is an employee or self-employed.

What is considered an employee or a self-employed (independent contractor)
Employee
A person who works in the service of another person (the employer) under an express or implied contract of hire, under which the employer has the right to control the details of work performance.
Self-employed worker (independent contractor)
One who is hired to undertake a specific project but who is left free to do the assigned work and to choose the method for accomplishing it. Unlike an employee, an independent contractor who commits a wrong while carrying out the work does not create liability for the one who did the hiring.
Indicators of the type of relationship
  • Indicators of an employee – employer relationship
    • The payer has the right to control what work the worker is to perform as well as how, when, and where the worker works
    • The payer sets the worker's hours of work
    • The payer determines the worker's rate of pay
    • The worker is paid by the hour, day or week rather than based on a ‘per-completion' basis
    • The working arrangement tends to be more continuous and exclusive
    • The payer provides benefits as part of the worker's compensation package
    • The worker is required to do the work themselve and cannot send a substitute
    • The worker provides only hand tools and the employer provides or rents the more expensive and larger tools and equipment
    • Employees receive training or direction from the employer
    • Employees can be fired
    • Employees can quit without recourse
    • Employees don't have a business presence
  • Indicators of a self-employed worker
    • The ability to choose when and for whom services are performed
    • The worker is hired to perform a specific task or part of the overall project
    • The worker does not have to perform the work themselves
    • The worker is paid based on completion
    • The worker is responsible to correct errors without additional pay
    • The worker is responsible to replace damaged materials at their own cost
    • The worker is hired for a specific job and does not have a continuous relationship
    • The worker can work on more than one project at a time
    • The worker determines who has first call on their time
    • The worker provides their own expensive or rented equipment
    • The worker is responsible for the maintenance and repairs to their equipment
    • The worker is liable for a penalty if the work is not completed on schedule
    • The worker has a business presence

  1. Contract arrangement

    1. At the time of contracting, how did the parties intend to characterize the relationship?

      • Employer-employee

        The two parties intend to enter into a contract of service.

      • Indipendent contractor

        The two parties intend to enter into a contract for services.

      Sometimes the intention is clear and both parties are in agreement (common intent). Sometimes the intent can be found in a written agreement. Sometimes the two parties have a different understanding as to the status of their working relationship, in which case there is no common intent.

      To decide the parties’ intentions, the CRA gets a copy of the contract, or testimony by the parties and examines the parties’ actions. Both parties’ intentions form part of the context of the employment that the CRA analyses.
