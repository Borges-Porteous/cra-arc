Factors to consider

You need to consider multiple factors to determine if the primary beneficiary is the employee or the employee. This includes whether other employees received the same benefit, if the benefit is part of the employee’s job duties, as well as if the benefit is for personal use. Use the factors to help you.

  1. Benefit

    Is the benefit something that can be used on an ongoing basis or something that can only be used once?

    • Ongoing
    • Once

    Generally,

  2. Recipient

    1. Who received the benefit?

      • Employee
      • Employee's family member or friend

      Generally, the employee is the primary beneficiary if a non-employee relative or friend used the benefit. Family members include spouses, children, siblings, parents, cousins, uncles, aunts, and grandparents.

    2. Was the benefit available to other employees with different duties?

      • Yes
      • No

      Generally, the employee is the primary beneficiary if the benefit is given to other employees with different duties. This could mean that the benefit was not tied to the employee's specific employment duties.

  3. Use in employment

    1. Did the employee's work productivity improve because of the benefit?

      • Yes
      • No

      Generally, whether a benefit improves the employee's work productivity depends in part on the employee's particular role and responsibilities.

    2. Would it be possible for the employee to do his or her job without the benefit or a replacement?

      • Yes
      • No

      Generally, the employer is the primary beneficiary if the job duties being fulfilled would not be possible without the benefit.

    3. Is it usual for employers in this industry to provide this benefit to employees to allow those employees to more effectively perform the duties of employment?

      • Yes
      • No

      Generally, the employer is the primary beneficiary if it is common in the industry to provide the benefit to allow employees to more effectively perform their duties. For example, when the benefit enhances productivity or makes it possible for the employee to do their job.

  4. Personal use

    1. Did the employer place restrictions on the use of the benefit?

      • Yes
      • No

      Generally, when the employer places restrictions on the use of the benefit it may mean that the employer is trying to contain the use to that which is beneficial to the employer. Depending on the situation, it may mean that the employer is the primary beneficiary. This includes restrictions on where and how the benefit can be used.

    2. Did the nature of the benefit significantly limit the opportunity for personal use?

      • Yes
      • No

      Generally, the employer is the primary beneficiary if the benefit provides limited opportunity for personal use. This could be considered as provided for business use.

    3. Was there in fact any personal use of the benefit?

      • Yes
      • No

      Generally, the employee is the primary beneficiary if the employee enjoyed personal use of the benefit. This includes situations were restrictions on the ability of the employee to use the benefit for personal use even if those restrictions are placed by the employer.

What if you do not know if the worker is an employee or self-employed

You can ask for a CPP/EI ruling. If you are not sure if the worker is an employee or self-employed, you can request a CPP/EI ruling.
