Calculation methods

Reminder: There are online calculators that will calculate the income tax deductions for you.

  • Regular payments (income tax tables)

Regular payments (tax tables)

Income tax tables calculate the required income tax for you on given ranges of income for a specified pay period.

Steps

  1. Determine if you can use income tax contribution tables

    Income tax tables can be used in most common situations.

    When to use or not to use the tax tables

    Use when

    • The payment is for regular employment income or was made with regular pay for one of the common or uncommon pay periods for which tables are provided
    • If you received Form TD1-IN (in addition to the TD1) from your employee, only use these tables on the portion of their income which is taxable

    Do not use when

    • The payment (for example, bonuses, commissions, retroactive pay) is made separately from regular pay
    • Your pay period does not have a table provided in the common or uncommon pay periods per year
    • You received Form TD1X from your employee
    • You received Form TD3F from the individual

  2. Review the completed TD1 forms to determine if you need to deduct tax

    Review the completed TD1 forms and verify if the employee checked the box “Total income less than total claim amount”.

    • If they checked this box, do not deduct any federal or provincial tax. Do not use the tax tables.
    • If they checked this box but you believe it is a false statement, continue to next step.

  3. Get the total claim amount from the TD1 forms

    1. Get the federal TD1 form

      Find the "TOTAL CLAIM AMOUNT" on the completed federal TD1 form

    2. Get the provincial or territorial TD1 form

      If they are claiming more than the basic personal amount, find the "TOTAL CLAIM AMOUNT" on the completed provincial or territorial TD1 form

Retroactive payments

In addition to regular types of payments, you must deduct income tax from retroactive payments.

Steps

  1. Determine if you can use the retroactive method

    • If the payment is made with regular pay, do not use the following steps to calculate. Use one of the following:

      • Payroll Deductions Online Calculator (PDOC)
      • Regular payments (income tax tables)

    • If the payment is made separately from regular pay, continue to next step

  2. Determine the calculation to use

    The calculation depends on your employee total remuneration for the year, including the increase. If your employee's total remuneration is:

    • Less than $5,000, continue to step 3
    • More than $5,000, continue to step 4

  3. Less than $5,000 - Calculate the amount of income tax you have to withhold and remit

    • Retroactive pay increase
    • multiply by 15% tax (10 % tax if you are in Quebec)
    • equals This amount is the income tax that you must withhold and remit from your employee's pay
    Calculation example

    Joseph's pay increased from $440 to $460 per week. His total remuneration for the year is $3,500. His province of employment is Manitoba.

    • Retroactive pay increase
    • multiply by 15% income tax
    • equals $XXX is the income tax that you must withhold and remit from Joseph's pay

    8. Calculation example

    Irene's pay increased from $440 to $460 per week. The increase was retroactive to 12 weeks, which gives her a total retroactive payment of $240 (12 × $20). Her province of employment is Nova Scotia. The claim code that applies to her TD1 and TD1NS forms is "6."

    In the payroll deductions tables, choose the weekly tables (52 pay periods a year) to find the increase in the weekly federal and provincial tax that you should deduct because of the increased pay rate.

    Find the federal and provincial tax that you deduct on $460 per week.

    Subtract the federal and provincial tax that you deduct on $440 per week.

    The result is the tax you have to deduct on the additional $20 per week.

    Multiply the increase in the weekly tax that you deduct by the number of weeks to which the retroactive pay increase applies.

    This amount is the tax that you must deduct from the retroactive payment.

Bonuses or irregular payments

In addition to regular types of payments, you must deduct income tax from bonuses or other types of irregular payments.

Steps

  1. Determine if you can use the bonus method

    • If the payment is made with regular pay, do not use the following steps to calculate. Use one of the following:

      • Payroll Deductions Online Calculator (PDOC)
      • Calculations of CPP, EI and income tax deductions using the tax tables

    • If the payment is made separately from regular pay, continue to next step

  2. Calculate the amount of income tax you have to withhold and remit

    Use the calculation depending on your employee total remuneration for the year (including the bonus or increase) and the frequency of when the bonus is paid within a year.

    Calculation - Employee total remuneration is $5,000 or less
    • Bonus payment
    • multiply by 15% tax (10 % tax if you are in Quebec)
    • equals This amount is the income tax that you must deduct from your employee's pay
    Calculation example
    Calculation - Employee total remuneration is more than $5,000 and it is the first or only bonus payment in the year
    • Bonus payment or the retroactive pay increase
    • divide by Number of pay periods in the year
    • equals Result
    • plus Employee’s salary, wages benefits, and allowances for the total period
    • equals This amount is the adjusted pay to use from the Federal, provincial and territorial income tax deduction tables

    4. Determine the amount of tax to deduct from the regular salary in the pay period

      8. Calculation example – First or once-a-year bonus payment

      Donna earns a salary of $400 per week. In September, you gave her a bonus of $300. Her province of employment is British Columbia. The claim code that applies to her TD1 and TD1BC forms is "1."

      Divide the bonus by the number of pay periods in the year ($300 ÷ 52 = $5.77).

      Add the $5.77 to the current pay rate of $400. As a result, the adjusted pay rate for the year is $405.77 per week.

      In the payroll deductions tables, choose the weekly tables (52 pay periods a year) to find the increased weekly federal and provincial tax you should deduct on the additional $5.77 per week.

      Find the federal and provincial tax that you deduct on $405.77 per week.

      Subtract the federal and provincial tax that you deduct on $400 per week.

      The result is the tax you have to deduct on the additional $5.77 per week.

      Multiply the additional tax you deduct per week by 52 (the number of pay periods in the year). This gives you the amount of income tax to deduct from the bonus of $300.

    3. Current bonus payment ÷ number of pay periods in the year
    4. Amount from Step 3 + previous adjusted pay rate (from step 2) = Total adjusted pay rate
    5. Get the tax deduction for the total adjusted pay rate (from Step 4) from the applicable federal and provincial tax table
    6. Determine the amount of tax to deduct from the previous adjusted pay rate (from step 2)
    7. Total adjusted pay tax deduction (from step 5) - previous adjusted pay deduction (from step 6)
    8. Multiply the result from step 7 by the number of pay periods in the year.
    9. This is the tax to be deducted from the second bonus payment.

      10. For any additional bonuses in the year, you need to follow the same steps and you need to take into account all previous payments.

      Calculation example – More than one bonus payment a year

      Mario earns a salary of $400 per week (amount 1). You paid him bonuses of $300 in January and $780 in February. His province of employment is Alberta. The claim code that applies to his TD1 and TD1AB forms is "1."

      The calculation must take into account all bonuses you paid during the year. You have to calculate the amount of tax to deduct for the entire year, regardless of when you paid the bonus.

      Divide the bonus you paid in January by the number of pay periods in the year ($300 ÷ 52 = $5.77) (amount 2). Add the $5.77 to the weekly salary of $400 to determine the adjusted weekly pay before the February bonus ($400 + $5.77 = $405.77).

      Divide the last bonus you paid to Mario by the number of pay periods in the year ($780 ÷ 52 = $15) (amount 3). Add amounts 1, 2, and 3 to determine the adjusted weekly pay for the year of $420.77 ($400 + $5.77 + $15).

      In the payroll deductions tables, choose the weekly tables (52 pay periods a year) to find the increased weekly federal and provincial tax you should deduct on the additional $15 per week.

      Find the federal and provincial tax that you deduct on $420.77 per week.

      Subtract the federal and provincial tax that you deduct on $405.77 per week.

      The result is the tax you have to deduct on the additional $15.

      Multiply the additional tax per week by 52 to determine the amount to deduct on the bonus of $780.

Any payments (manual calculation)

Manual calculation can also be used for all payments made to employees to calculate the income tax contribution that must be withheld for the pay period.

Steps

  1. Calculate the annual taxable income

    • Employee’s gross remuneration for the pay period
    • plus Employee's taxable benefits and allowances for the period
    • minus Employee’s earnings:
      • Deduction for living in a prescribed zone
      • Amount a tax services office has authorized
      • Employee’s contributions to a registered pension plan (RPP)
      • Union dues
      • Employee’s contributions to a retirement compensation arrangement (RCA) or certain pension plans
      • Employee’s and employer’s contributions (deducted at source) to a registered retirement savings plan (RRSP) provided you have reasonable grounds to believe the employee can deduct the contribution for the year
      • Employee contributions to a pooled registered pension plan (PRPP), or a similar provincial pension plan, as long as the plan is registered with the Minister of National Revenue and you have reasonable grounds to believe the employee can deduct the contribution for the year
      • First and second additional contributions to the CPP made by the employee
    • equals This is the net remuneration for the pay period
    • multiply by Number of pay periods in the year
    • equals This is the annual net income
    • minus Annual deduction for living in a prescribed zone, reported on federal form TD1
    • equals This is the annual taxable income for the employee
