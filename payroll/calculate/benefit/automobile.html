  1. Determine if a motor vehicle is an automobile

    The CRA defines automobiles and motor vehicles differently for tax purposes.

    Answer a few questions to determine if the vehicle is an automobile or a motor vehicle.

    Launch vehicle validator

    Vehicle validator

    Automobile and motor vehicle definitions
    Car with seating for the driver and up to four passengers

    The vehicle is a car with seating for the driver and up to four passengers. The vehicle is:

    • not used primarily as a taxi
    • not bought to sell, rent, or lease in a sales, rental or leasing business

    The vehicle is considered a motor vehicle if they do not meet all conditions.

    The vehicle is considered an automobile if they meet all conditions.

    Vehicle designed or adapted for transporting up to eight passengers

    The vehicle is designed or adapted for transporting up to eight passengers. The vehicle is:

    • not bought to be used primarily as a taxi
    • not bought to be used primarily in a business of transporting passengers
    • not bought to sell, rent, or lease in a sales, rental or leasing business

    The vehicle is considered a motor vehicle if they do not meet all conditions.

    The vehicle is considered an automobile if they meet all conditions.

    Emergency response vehicle (police, fire or medical)

    The vehicle is an emergency response vehicle. The vehicle is:

    • an ambulance
    • clearly marked as a police or fire emergency response vehicle
    • clearly marked as an emergency medical response vehicle that you use to carry emergency medical equipment and one or more emergency medical attendants or paramedics

    The vehicle is considered a motor vehicle if they do not meet all conditions.

    The vehicle is considered an automobile if they meet all conditions.

    Pick-up truck

    The vehicle is a pick-up truck. The vehicle is:

    • used at a remote work location or at a special work site primarily to transport goods or equipment in the course of business (at least 30 kilometres away from any community having a population of at least 40,000)
    • can seat equal or less than the driver and two passengers and you used it primarily to transport goods or equipment in the course of business
    • used exclusively to transport goods or equipment in the course of business

    The vehicle is considered a motor vehicle if they do not meet all conditions.

    The vehicle is considered an automobile if they meet all conditions.

    Van, minivan, sport utility or similar vehicle

    The vehicle is van, minivan, sport utility or similar vehicle. The vehicle is:

    • can seat equal or less than the driver and two passengers and you used it primarily to transport goods or equipment in the course of business
    • used exclusively to transport goods or equipment in the course of business

    The vehicle is considered a motor vehicle if they do not meet all conditions.

    The vehicle is considered an automobile if they meet all conditions.

    Hearse or another vehicle used in a funeral business to transport passengers

    The vehicle is a hearse or another vehicle used in a funeral business to transport passengers. The vehicle is:

    • is a hearse, it is used primarily in a funeral business
    • not a hearse, you bought it to use a funeral business to transport passengers

    The vehicle is considered a motor vehicle if they do not meet all conditions.

    The vehicle is considered an automobile if they meet all conditions.

  2. Determine if a benefit is taxable

    Generally, if your employee uses one of your automobile it is a taxable benefit. Depending on your situation, the benefit may not be taxable under the CRA's administrative policy .

    Non-taxable situation

    Under the CRA's administrative policy, if you provide your employee with an automobile that you own or lease and your employee uses the automobile for business use only, the benefit is not taxable.

    Taxable situation

    If you provide your employee with an automobile that you own or lease and your employee uses the automobile for both business and personal purposes, the personal use is a taxable benefit.

    Continue to step 3

  3. Determine if the employee is using the automobile for personal use and what records to keep

    The personal use is when the employee's use of an automobile provided by the employer or a person related to the employer, for purposes other than the employer's business.

    Keeping records of business and personal use

    To show business or personal use of a vehicle, you and your employee must keep a record of the vehicle's use. The total kilometres travelled by the vehicle in a calendar year must be broken down between personal and business use.

    Motor vehicle expenses can only be deducted when they are reasonable and supporting documentation, such as receipts, is provided. The employee must record odometer readings in the log at the start and the end of each vehicle use.

    This is important when purchasing, selling, or trading an employer-owned vehicle.

    A record or log for entering kilometres travelled for business purposes must include the following information for each trip:

    • Date of the trip
    • Destination
    • Purpose of the trip
    • Number of kilometres travelled

    If your employee goes to several destinations in a day, they must enter the details for each trip.

    • Business use

      • Travel from office workplace to client workplace (and vice versa)
      • Travel from office workplace for errands for business purposes (and vice versa)
      • Travel from home to client workplace (and vice versa)

    • Personal use

      • Travel from office workplace to home (and vice versa), even if the employer insist the employee to drive the vehicle home, for example:
        • the employee is on 24 hour call
        • the employer feels the security of the vehicle and its contents is better served at the employee's residence
        • it is more convenient or economical to have the vehicle driven to the employee's residence
      • Vacation trips

  4. Calculate standby charge for automobile

    Reminder: You can use Automobile Benefits Online Calculator that will calculate the benefit for you.

    The standby charge is calculated based on the number of kilometres travelled for personal purposes with the employer's automobile in a calendar year. The value of the standby charge is also based on the cost to the employer of purchasing or leasing the automobile.

    Examples of when a standby charge benefit applies or does not apply

    • Example 1

      Employee is a technician who works for a local telephone company. The company permanently provides the employee with a service vehicle, unit 333. At the beginning of each work day, the employee uses unit 333 to conduct the service visits assigned to them. At the end of each day, the employee returns unit 333 to the company's parking lot and returns the keys to the employer.

      • The automobile is made available during working hours
      • The automobile is only used for business purposes

      The employee does not receive any automobile standby charge benefit.

    • Example 2

      Employee is a regional manager for the same telephone company. Unit 222 is permanently assigned to the employee. One of the requirements of the employee's position requires them to travel abroad on a regular basis. As a result, the employee keeps the keys to the vehicle throughout the year. When travelling abroad, the employee leaves unit 222 at the airport. The employee also uses the vehicle to travel between their home and their workplace.

      • The automobile is made available throughout the year
      • The automobile is used for business and personal purposes

      The employee receives an automobile stanby charge benefit.

    • Example 3

      As a work incentive, the employer makes an automobile available to an employee's spouse, to be driven for personal use.

      • The automobile is made available throughout the year
      • The automobile is used for personal purposes to an employee's spouse

      The employee receives an automobile stanby charge benefit (and not the spouse).

    1. Determine if the employer required the employee to use the automobile for their work

      You need to determine if the employer required the employee to use the automobile for their work.

      • The benefit provided to the employee meets the condition 1 of 3 for the reduced standby charge if the employer required the employee to use the vehicle as part of the duties of an office or employment

      • The employee will not benefit from the reduction if the employer did not require the employee to use the vehicle as part of the duties of an office or employment

    2. Calculate the percentage of the employee's personal use of the automobile

      • Kilometres driven for personal use
      • divide by Total kilometres driven for business and personal use
      • equals Result
      • multiply by 100
      • equals This is the % of personal use of the automobile

      • The benefit provided to the employee meets the condition 2 of 3 for the reduced standby charge if the automobile is used 50% or more (primarily) for business use

      • The employee will not benefit from the reduction if the automobile is used 50% or less (primarily) for business use

      Calculation example

      Judith has a total mileage of 18,000 kilometres driven and she used the automobile for 1,800 kilometres for personal purposes spread throught the year.

      • 1,800 is the kilometres driven for personal use
      • divide by 18,000 is the total kilometres driven for business and personal use
      • equals 0.10 is the result
      • multiply by 100
      • equals 10% is the personal use of the automobile

    3. Calculate the limit that applies to the number of kilometres travelled for personal use

      Calculate the kilometres travelled for personal use.

      • Total number of days when the automobile was available to the employee
      • divide by 30 days
      • equals Result (rounded to the nearest whole number)
      • multiply by 1,667 km
      • equals Total prorated kilometre for personal use

      If the employer and the employee kept detailed records confirming that less than 1,667 km were travelled per month for personal use (20,004 km per year), the benefit provided to the employee meets the condition 3 of 3 for the reduced standby charge.

      Records that must be kept

      The employer and employee must keep records containing enough detail to determine:

      • the total number of days in the year during which the vehicle was available to the employee
      • the total number of kilometres that the vehicle travelled in the calendar year
      • the total number of kilometres travelled for personal use in the year
      • all reimbursements made by the employee for the use of the vehicle

      If no record is kept, the average limit of 1,667 km per month will be used in the calculation. This means that the employee will not benefit from the reduction.

      Calculation example

      An automobile owned (or leased) by the employer is made available to the employee for 185 days in the last taxation year

      • 185 is the total number of days when the automobile was available to the employee
      • divide by 30 days
      • equals 6 is the result (6.167 rounded to the nearest whole number)
      • multiply by 1,667 km
      • equals 10,002 is the total prorated kilometre for personal use

      • equals Result

      • equals Result
      • multiply by Number of months available for use
      • equals Result

  7. Withhold payroll deductions and remit GST/HST

    If the benefit is taxable, you must withhold the following deductions. The amounts must be included in the pay period they were received or enjoyed.

    The withholding and remitting requirement depends on the type of remuneration: cash , non-cash , or near-cash .

    • Non-cash and near-cash: Option 1

      Withhold:

      • Income tax
      • CPP
      • EI (do not withhold)

      Remit:

      • GST/HST

    • Cash: Option 2

      Withhold:

      • Income tax
      • CPP
      • EI

      Remit:

      • GST/HST - Reimbursements
      • GST/HST - Allowances (do not remit)

  8. Report the benefit on a slip

    If the benefit is taxable, you must report the following on the T4 slip.

    • Non-cash and near-cash: Option 1

      Report on:

      • Box 14 - Employment Income
      • Box 26 - CPP/QPP pensionable earnings
      • Code 40 - Other Information

    • Cash: Option 2

      Report on:

      • Box 14 - Employment Income
      • Box 24 - EI insurable earnings
      • Box 26 - CPP/QPP pensionable earnings
      • Code 40 - Other Information
