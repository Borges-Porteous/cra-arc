    Definition – Type of vehicles

    Car with seating for the driver and up to four passengers

    The vehicle is a car with seating for the driver and up to four passengers. The vehicle is:

    • not used primarily as a taxi
    • not bought to sell, rent, or lease in a sales, rental or leasing business

    The vehicle is considered a motor vehicle if they do not meet all conditions.

    The vehicle is considered an automobile if they meet all conditions.

    Vehicle designed or adapted for transporting up to eight passengers

    The vehicle is designed or adapted for transporting up to eight passengers. The vehicle is:

    • not bought to be used primarily as a taxi
    • not bought to be used primarily in a business of transporting passengers
    • not bought to sell, rent, or lease in a sales, rental or leasing business

    The vehicle is considered a motor vehicle if they do not meet all conditions.

    The vehicle is considered an automobile if they meet all conditions.

    Emergency response vehicle (police/fire/medical)

    The vehicle is an emergency response vehicle. The vehicle is:

    • an ambulance
    • clearly marked as a police or fire emergency response vehicle
    • clearly marked as an emergency medical response vehicle that you use to carry emergency medical equipment and one or more emergency medical attendants or paramedics

    The vehicle is considered a motor vehicle if they do not meet all conditions.

    The vehicle is considered an automobile if they meet all conditions.

    Pick-up truck

    The vehicle is a pick-up truck. The vehicle is:

    • used at a remote work location or at a special work site primarily to transport goods or equipment in the course of business (at least 30 kilometres away from any community having a population of at least 40,000)
    • can seat equal or less than the driver and two passengers and you used it primarily to transport goods or equipment in the course of business
    • used exclusively to transport goods or equipment in the course of business

    The vehicle is considered a motor vehicle if they do not meet all conditions.

    The vehicle is considered an automobile if they meet all conditions.

    Van, minivan, sport utility or similar vehicle

    The vehicle is van, minivan, sport utility or similar vehicle. The vehicle is:

    • can seat equal or less than the driver and two passengers and you used it primarily to transport goods or equipment in the course of business
    • used exclusively to transport goods or equipment in the course of business

    The vehicle is considered a motor vehicle if they do not meet all conditions.

    The vehicle is considered an automobile if they meet all conditions.

    Hearse or another vehicle used in a funeral business to transport passengers

    The vehicle is a hearse or another vehicle used in a funeral business to transport passengers. The vehicle is:

    • is a hearse, it is used primarily in a funeral business
    • not a hearse, you bought it to use a funeral business to transport passengers

    The vehicle is considered a motor vehicle if they do not meet all conditions.

    The vehicle is considered an automobile if they meet all conditions.

  2. Determine if a benefit is taxable

    Your employee may use an employer-owned or leased automobile for both business and personal purposes. The personal use of the automobile is considered a taxable benefit for the employee.

    Situations

    Situation: Automobile you provide to an employee

    Non-taxable situation

    Under the CRA's administrative policy, if you provide your employee with an automobile that you own and your employee uses the automobile for business use only (part of their employment duties), the fair market value (FMV) of the automobile expenses is not taxable.

    Taxable situation

    If the benefit does not meet all of the conditions above, it is a taxable benefit. When you provide your employee with an automobile for personal use, the benefit is taxable. Generally, the benefit you provide includes a standby charge and an operating expense benefit for the year.

  3. Calculate the value of the benefit

    Reminder: You can use Automobile Benefits Online Calculator that will calculate the benefit for you.

    What are the reasonable automobile allowance rates

    The automobile allowance rates for 2022 are:

    • 61¢ per kilometre for the first 5,000 kilometres driven
    • 55¢ per kilometre driven after that
      In the Northwest Territories, Yukon, and Nunavut, there is an additional 4¢ per kilometre allowed for travel.

    The automobile allowance rates for 2021 are:

    • 59¢ per kilometre for the first 5,000 kilometres driven
    • 53¢ per kilometre driven after that
      In the Northwest Territories, Yukon, and Nunavut, there is an additional 4¢ per kilometre allowed for travel.

    The automobile allowance rates for 2020 are:

    • 59¢ per kilometre for the first 5,000 kilometres driven
    • 53¢ per kilometre driven after that
      In the Northwest Territories, Yukon, and Nunavut, there is an additional 4¢ per kilometre allowed for travel.

    The automobile allowance rates for 2019 are:

    • 58¢ per kilometre for the first 5,000 kilometres driven
    • 52¢ per kilometre driven after that
      In the Northwest Territories, Yukon, and Nunavut, there is an additional 4¢ per kilometre allowed for travel.

    The automobile allowance rates for 2018 are:

    • 55¢ per kilometre for the first 5,000 kilometres driven
    • 49¢ per kilometre driven after that
      In the Northwest Territories, Yukon, and Nunavut, there is an additional 4¢ per kilometre allowed for travel.

    The automobile allowance rates for 2016 and 2017 are:

    • 54¢ per kilometre for the first 5,000 kilometres driven
    • 48¢ per kilometre driven after that
      In the Northwest Territories, Yukon, and Nunavut, there is an additional 4¢ per kilometre allowed for travel.

    The automobile allowance rates for 2015 are:

    • 55¢ per kilometre for the first 5,000 kilometres driven
    • 49¢ per kilometre driven after that
      In the Northwest Territories, Yukon, and Nunavut, there is an additional 4¢ per kilometre allowed for travel.

  4. Withhold payroll deductions and remit GST/HST

    If the benefit is taxable, you must withhold the following deductions. The amounts must be included in the pay period they were received or enjoyed.

    The withholding and remitting requirement depends on the type of remuneration: cash , non-cash , or near-cash .

    • Non-cash and near-cash: Option 1

      Withhold:

      • Income tax
      • CPP
      • EI (do not withhold)

      Remit:

      • GST/HST

    • Cash: Option 2

      Withhold:

      • Income tax
      • CPP
      • EI

      Remit:

      • GST/HST - Reimbursements
      • GST/HST - Allowances (do not remit)

  5. Report the benefit on a slip

    If the benefit is taxable, you must report the following on the T4 slip.

    • Non-cash and near-cash: Option 1

      Report on:

      • Box 14 - Employment Income
      • Box 26 - CPP/QPP pensionable earnings
      • Code 40 - Other Information

    • Cash: Option 2

      Report on:

      • Box 14 - Employment Income
      • Box 24 - EI insurable earnings
      • Box 26 - CPP/QPP pensionable earnings
      • Code 40 - Other Information
