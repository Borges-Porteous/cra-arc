Fundamental requirements of SR&ED work

SR&ED work takes place in the fields of science and technology. Eligible work must meet certain fundamental requirements to be considered scientific research and experimental development.

Your reason for doing the work (why you started it) and the way in which you approac the work (how you carry it out) will help determine if the work is in fact SR&ED.

Eligible SR&ED work must include the 3 following items:

  • A scientific or technological uncertainty that you tried to overcome
  • A systematic investigation or search that you carried out to overcome the uncertainty
  • An advancement that you achieved or tried to achieve

The uncertainty

SR&ED work must be based on a scientific or technological uncertainty that you are trying to overcome.

The uncertainty is the reason you begin the SR&ED work. It is the result of an issue you encounter in your work that could not be resolved with the generally available scientific or technological knowledge.

The uncertainty may exist because of current shortcomings or limitations in science and technology. By trying to resolve it, you’re working towards a scientific or technological advancement.

You will need to be able to explain the gap in the existing knowledge at the time you started the work and your attempt to resolve it.  

The investigation or [re]search

The work you do in the tax year to try and overcome the uncertainty must be carried out through experiment and analysis. It is not a matter of using known design methods or established techniques.

The work must be a systematic investigation or [re]search.

You must be able to explain the work that was done or planned and describe how you gained the knowledge you needed to reach your goal.

What a systematic investigation includes
  1. The hypothesis:
    1. The idea that triggered your investigation to overcome the uncertainty.
    2. You may express this as a possible solution to a problem, a proposed method, or an approach.
  2. The experiment:
    1. the experiment and/or analysis conducted to test your hypothesis/ idea. This may change or evolve as a result of the testing.
  3. The results obtained:
    1. The results or findings of the experiment or analysis
  4. The conclusions:
    1. the logical conclusions based on the results

The advancement

The advancement is the increase in knowledge that your SR&ED work achieved. It’s how the work improved the scientific or technological knowledge base that existed at the start of the work.

By increasing scientific or technological understanding, the advancement should move the knowledge base of your business to a higher level.  

Keep in mind that it’s the advancement in the underlying science or technology that is important, not how the work advanced your business or business practices.

You do not have to achieve your objective to gain new knowledge.

For example, if your work allowed you to understand that the idea you tested is not a solution for your situation, this can be considered new knowledge.

Eligible research and development work

Work that is eligible for SR&ED tax incentives must be in the fields of science or technology and must be carried out in Canada. Eligible types of work include the following:

Basic research

The work you’re doing is working towards increasing the scientific or technological knowledge base. Your work often takes place in laboratories and may be published in scientific journals.

An example of basic research is the study of a newly discovered virus to better understand its characteristics.

Applied research

The work you’re doing also increases scientific or technological knowledge, but has a specific practical application in view. Like basic research, your work often takes place in laboratories and may be published in scientific journals.   

An example of applied research would be efforts to develop a vaccine against a new virus by exploiting some identified characteristics of that newly discovered virus.

Experimental development

The work your doing increases scientific or technological knowledge from research and more practical experience. Your work is attempting to develop new or improved materials, devices, products or processes.

An example of when experimental development could occur might be during the scale up and optimization of processes to enable large scale and rapid production of a vaccine.

Eligible support work

Support work is not SR&ED, but it may be eligible if it corresponds and directly supports the SR&ED work you’re doing.

For example, if you are developing a vaccine, you may perform tests on the virus to observe its characteristics. This testing would be considered support work of your basic research.

Types of eligible support work:

  • engineering
  • design
  • operations research
  • mathematical analysis
  • computer programming
  • data collection
  • testing
  • psychological research

Work that is not eligible

Some work is specifically excluded from being eligible for SR&ED tax incentives. Any work that is not conducted specifically to overcome the uncertainty is not eligible.

Review types of excluded work

The following work is not eligible or considered support work:

  • Market research or sales promotion
  • Quality control or routine testing of materials, devices, products or processes
  • Research in the social sciences or the humanities
  • Prospecting, exploring or drilling for, or producing, minerals, petroleum or natural gas
  • The commercial production of a new or improved material, device or product or the commercial use of a new or improved process
  • Style changes
  • Routine data collection
  • Acquiring available knowledge or know-how (for example, through training)
  • On-the-job learning, hiring expert employees or consultants, or purchasing proprietary knowledge

If you receive other funding

If you receive R&D tax credits through your province or territory, you may still claim SR&ED tax incentives, though it may reduce the amount you receive.

You must report any financial assistance you receive in your SR&ED expenditures claim.
